\section{Single-Level Multiobjective Formulation}
The single-level formulation used in this project should be read as a specialization of the framework introduced in Chapters 2 and 3 of the TFG. In the general public-network setting, the model is posed as a continuous optimization problem that minimizes a weighted combination of passenger cost and operator cost,
\begin{equation}
\min \; \alpha \, PAX + (1-\alpha)\, OP,
\end{equation}
where the scalar parameter $\alpha \in (0,1)$ balances the two objectives. The passenger term $PAX$ contains the negative utilities, the entropy regularizers that generate the logit split, and the congestion surrogates, whereas the operator term $OP$ contains the operating cost of the designed network. Fixed deployment costs are first expressed through indicator functions in a budget constraint and are then incorporated into the objective through a Lagrange multiplier $\beta$ together with a reweighted $\ell_1$ approximation.

The TFG also shows that this leads to a penalized objective of the form
\begin{equation}
\alpha\,PAX + (1-\alpha)\,OP + \beta\,\Phi_{\mathrm{deploy}}^{\mathrm{rw}},
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_{\mathrm{deploy}}^{\mathrm{rw}}$ is the MM-based reweighted approximation of the fixed and linear deployment costs. This is the continuous single-level template on top of which the airline model is built.

For the airline application, the objective changes in an important way. Since the operator is private, the model is no longer written as a social compromise between passengers and operator. Instead, the single-level airline objective combines:
\begin{itemize}
\item operating costs on active flight legs;
\item reweighted deployment costs associated with airport capacity, hub capacity, and, in the original TFG formulation, also link activation;
\item passenger-utility terms on the new airline network;
\item passenger-utility terms on the external competing alternative;
\item entropy terms that recover a logit market split;
\item congestion penalties associated with airport slack variables.
\end{itemize}

The most characteristic modification of the airline model, emphasized in Chapter 5, is that utility and entropy terms are multiplied by market prices. This is done so that the lower-level problem is no longer merely attracted to large-demand markets, but instead gives more importance to markets with greater revenue potential. In other words, the entropic logit structure is preserved, but it is embedded into a profit-oriented design model.

The continuous airline formulation is completed with the main operational constraints already reflected in the code:
\begin{itemize}
\item link-capacity constraints tying passenger flows to flight frequencies through nominal aircraft capacity and load factor;
\item airport-capacity constraints for departures and arrivals;
\item hub-capacity constraints for transfer traffic;
\item symmetry constraints on operated links;
\item flow-conservation constraints for every market;
\item market-share constraints $f_{od}+f^{\mathrm{ext}}_{od}=1$;
\item profitability constraints ensuring that captured markets are economically viable.
\end{itemize}

This is the true single-level basis of the present work. The bilevel scheme introduced later does not replace this model; it modifies the way market importance is encoded inside its passenger-related terms.
