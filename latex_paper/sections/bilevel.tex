\section{Bilevel Weight-Adjustment Framework}
The bilevel contribution of the present project starts from the single-level airline model just described and asks a natural question: how should the lower-level market weights be chosen so that the resulting design maximizes the airline's true profit? In the TFG, the market coefficients $w^{od}$ are treated as exogenous quantities derived from the data. In the current implementation, they are generalized to learnable coefficients of the form
\begin{equation}
w^{od}(\alpha_{od},\beta_{od}) = \alpha_{od}\bigl(p^{od}q^{od}\bigr)^{\beta_{od}},
\end{equation}
where $q^{od}$ and $p^{od}$ denote market demand and price. In the code, these matrices are stored as \texttt{alfa\_od} and \texttt{beta\_od}.

This introduces a notational overlap with the scalar parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ used in the TFG for multiobjective weighting and budget penalization. Conceptually, however, the roles are different. The scalar $\alpha$ of the TFG controls the trade-off between $PAX$ and $OP$ in the public-network model, whereas the matrices $\alpha_{od}$ and $\beta_{od}$ used here calibrate the relative relevance of each market inside the private-airline lower-level objective.

At a conceptual level, the extended problem can be written as
\begin{align}
\max_{\alpha_{od},\beta_{od}} \quad & \Pi\bigl(x^\star(\alpha_{od},\beta_{od})\bigr) \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & x^\star(\alpha_{od},\beta_{od}) \in \arg\min_x \Phi_{\mathrm{air}}(x;\alpha_{od},\beta_{od}),
\end{align}
where $\Phi_{\mathrm{air}}$ denotes the continuous airline single-level objective and $\Pi$ is the actual profit of the airline. The upper-level problem therefore does not redesign the network directly; instead, it learns how the lower level should value each market so that the induced design is more profitable.

The interpretation is straightforward. If a market contributes strongly to revenue and profitability but is underemphasized in the lower-level design, the bilevel update should increase its weight. If a market receives excessive emphasis in the lower-level objective relative to its profit contribution, its weight should be reduced. In this way, the bilevel layer acts as a calibration mechanism over the continuous design model inherited from the TFG.

In the current implementation, this calibration is not solved through an exact KKT-based bilevel reformulation. Instead, the matrices $\alpha_{od}$ and $\beta_{od}$ are updated iteratively using gradient-style corrections computed from successive lower-level solves. This choice is consistent with the philosophy of the original continuous approach: preserve tractability, avoid mixed-integer bilevel structure, and exploit repeated convex or nearly convex solves to approach a profit-oriented design.
