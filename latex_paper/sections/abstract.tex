\begin{abstract}
This work builds on a continuous optimization framework for transport-network design in which discrete construction decisions are replaced by sparse capacity variables and passenger choice is modeled through entropy regularization. In the underlying single-level formulation, fixed deployment costs are approximated through an iterative reweighted $\ell_1$ scheme inspired by majorization-minimization, while entropic terms are introduced so that the optimality conditions recover a logit-type market split between the designed network and the outside alternative. This yields a tractable continuous approximation to network design that preserves the main coupling among capacity, routing, congestion, and demand capture.

Starting from that framework, we focus on the airline hub-and-spoke case with a private operator. The single-level model includes operating costs, reweighted deployment costs, passenger-utility terms, entropy terms, and hub-specific capacity constraints. In contrast with the public-network formulation, the passenger terms are weighted by market prices so that the lower-level problem is aligned with revenue generation, and additional profitability constraints ensure that only economically viable markets are operated.

On top of this single-level model, we introduce a bilevel extension in which market weights are no longer fixed \emph{a priori}. Instead, the upper level seeks to maximize the real objective, namely airline profit, by tuning market-specific hyperparameters that scale the lower-level utility and entropy terms. To preserve tractability in the presence of fixed costs, the bilevel scheme retains the sparse continuous representation of deployment decisions and iteratively updates the reweighting coefficients using the capacities obtained in previous solves. The resulting methodology combines entropic logit demand allocation, continuous hub-and-spoke design, and bilevel calibration of market priorities within a unified framework suitable for airline network design problems that are too large for exact mixed-integer bilevel optimization.
\end{abstract}
