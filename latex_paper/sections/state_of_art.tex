\section{State of the Art}
Classical airline hub-and-spoke design models are usually formulated as mixed-integer network design problems. Binary variables decide whether an airport is activated, whether it operates as a hub, and whether a direct connection is offered, while continuous variables describe traffic assignment and frequencies. These formulations are expressive and remain the standard modeling choice when the problem size is moderate, but they become extremely demanding when fixed costs, multicommodity flows, demand choice, and realistic operational constraints are included simultaneously.

A second relevant line of work concerns demand modeling. In many network design models, passenger demand is treated either through deterministic all-or-nothing assignment or through logit-based market shares. The latter is more realistic in competitive settings, but it often leads to non-convex constraints or to piecewise-linear mixed-integer approximations. This substantially increases the computational burden and makes the resulting formulations difficult to scale.

These limitations have motivated continuous alternatives in which discrete activation decisions are replaced by sparse capacity variables and logit behavior is recovered through convex regularization. In that family of approaches, two ideas are particularly useful. First, reweighted $\ell_1$ penalties derived from logarithmic sparsity surrogates can emulate fixed activation costs while preserving a continuous optimization structure. Second, entropy terms in the objective can induce logit demand shares through first-order optimality conditions, avoiding the explicit enforcement of non-convex logit constraints.

The present work combines those ideas in the airline hub-and-spoke setting and extends them with a bilevel layer. The lower level remains a continuous network design model, but the market weights entering the passenger terms are now learned rather than fixed. For the resulting constrained bilevel problem, our resolution strategy follows the recent penalty-method viewpoint advocated in the bilevel optimization literature, where upper- and lower-level objectives are coupled through penalized single-level surrogates that can be treated by repeated first-order or gradient-based updates.
