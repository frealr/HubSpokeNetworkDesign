\section{Introduction}
The point of departure of this paper is the continuous design framework developed in Chapters 2, 3, and 5 of the accompanying TFG. That framework was introduced as an alternative to classical mixed-integer formulations for transport-network design, where binary variables are typically used to decide whether stations, links, or hubs are built. While those formulations are natural from a modeling viewpoint, they rapidly become difficult to solve when they are combined with multicommodity flows, passenger-choice models, congestion effects, and fixed deployment costs.

The key idea of the TFG is to recover much of that modeling richness without keeping the combinatorial structure explicitly. Chapter 2 provides the two technical ingredients that make this possible. The first is a \emph{sparsity}-promoting construction based on reweighted $\ell_1$ penalties and a majorization-minimization procedure, which is used to approximate fixed activation costs through continuous capacity variables. The second is an entropy-based reformulation of passenger choice: instead of imposing a non-convex logit constraint, entropic terms are added to the objective so that the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions reproduce the desired logit market shares at the optimum.

Chapter 3 then combines both ingredients into a general continuous single-level formulation for transport-network design. In that model, passenger disutility, operator cost, congestion, and budget effects are coupled through a convex program whose objective can be interpreted as a weighted trade-off between passenger and operator interests. Chapter 5 specializes that structure to the airline case, where the operator is private, the network follows a hub-and-spoke architecture, and the natural design objective is no longer social welfare but economic performance.

The work developed in this repository starts from that airline single-level model and extends it in a bilevel direction. More precisely, we keep the continuous lower-level structure inherited from the TFG and introduce market-specific hyperparameters that reweight the lower-level passenger terms. These hyperparameters are adjusted so that the induced network design better matches the true upper-level target, namely profit maximization. The contribution of this draft is therefore not to replace the TFG formulation, but to make explicit how the bilevel scheme grows on top of it.

The remainder of the paper follows that logic. We first summarize the airline hub-and-spoke setting and the variables used in the implementation. We then revisit the single-level continuous formulation from the TFG, explain how it is modified in the airline case, describe the bilevel extension based on market-wise weights, and finally outline the iterative computational scheme implemented in the code.
