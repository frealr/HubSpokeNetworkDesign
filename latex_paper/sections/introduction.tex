\section{Introduction}
Transportation networks are a critical component of modern societies. They support economic activity, territorial cohesion, access to employment and services, and the movement of passengers and goods at multiple spatial and temporal scales. From an optimization viewpoint, transportation network design seeks to determine which infrastructure should be deployed, how much capacity should be assigned to each component, and how the resulting network should be operated so that a given performance criterion is optimized under physical, economic, and service constraints.

Within this broad class of problems, hub-and-spoke networks play a particularly important role in air transportation. Structurally, they can be interpreted as the union of several star-shaped subnetworks interconnected through a reduced set of hub airports. Spoke airports mainly feed or receive local traffic, whereas hubs concentrate transfer activity and allow the operator to connect many origin-destination markets with a comparatively limited number of direct links. For airlines, this architecture is attractive because it simplifies operations, allows traffic consolidation, improves the use of aircraft and airport resources, and often provides a more robust operating structure under disruptions. Consequently, hub-and-spoke network design for a private airline is fundamentally a profit-driven problem in which the key decisions are the location of hubs, the activation of links, the assignment of capacities, and the routing of demand.

Classical formulations address these decisions through mixed-integer programming models, since infrastructure deployment and hub activation are naturally binary in nature. These models are expressive, but their complexity grows very rapidly once fixed costs, multicommodity flows, and realistic operating restrictions are included. Demand modeling creates an additional challenge. Simplified all-or-nothing policies are often too crude to represent passenger behavior, especially in competitive markets. By contrast, logit-based demand models are widely accepted because they allow the captured share of each market to vary smoothly with the perceived utility of the available alternatives. However, when embedded directly into a network design problem, they usually lead to hard-to-handle nonlinear or mixed-integer constraints.

The contribution of this paper is to combine several ideas into a single tractable framework. First, we adopt a continuous optimization model in which sparse capacity variables and reweighted $\ell_1$ penalties are used to approximate the fixed costs associated with airport, hub, and route activation. Second, we introduce a market-regularized lower-level objective whose entropy terms recover the desired logit market shares without explicit non-convex share constraints. Third, we embed this continuous model into a bilevel structure in which market coefficients are adjusted so that the induced network better matches the real objective of the airline, namely profit maximization. Finally, we solve the resulting model through a penalty-based reformulation, together with an outer iteration that updates the reweighted sparsity approximation.

The paper is organized as follows. After this introduction, we review the state of the art on hub-and-spoke design, continuous approximations of fixed-cost network design, and demand modeling. We then present the airline problem setting and its main variables. Next, we discuss the classical MIP viewpoint and the continuous convex reformulation that motivates our lower-level model. On that basis, we introduce the bilevel reformulation and the corresponding solution algorithm. Finally, we report computational experience, including experiments on small and medium-size networks for comparison against MIP benchmarks and larger network experiments that assess the validity of the proposed methodology in realistic settings.
