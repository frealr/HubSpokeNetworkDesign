\section{Introduction}
Airline hub-and-spoke design requires deciding which airports should operate as transfer centers, which direct connections should be offered, how much capacity should be assigned to each airport and link, and how origin-destination demand should be routed through the resulting network. These decisions are tightly coupled. Modifying the hub structure changes the set of feasible itineraries, which changes passenger utility and market capture, which in turn alters the profit associated with each route and the value of investing in additional capacity.

This interaction makes exact formulations difficult to solve at realistic scales. Mixed-integer models can represent route opening, hub activation, and fixed costs naturally, but they become computationally challenging once they are combined with multicommodity flows and demand-choice models. In competitive airline markets, the difficulty is even greater because the new airline must be designed while accounting for outside alternatives and for the fact that different markets contribute differently to profit.

This paper adopts a continuous optimization viewpoint. Instead of modeling build-or-not-build decisions with binary variables, we use sparse capacity variables and reweighted penalties to emulate fixed costs. Instead of imposing logit demand capture through non-convex constraints, we recover it through entropy terms in the objective. The result is a single-level continuous model that remains rich enough to represent the economic structure of airline hub-and-spoke design.

Our main contribution is to extend that formulation to a bilevel setting. The lower level is a continuous airline network design model with price-weighted utility and entropy terms, reweighted deployment costs, and hub-capacity restrictions. The upper level maximizes profit by learning market-specific hyperparameters that determine how strongly each origin-destination market is represented inside the lower-level passenger terms. In this sense, the bilevel layer calibrates the lower-level model so that the network induced by continuous optimization is better aligned with the true business objective of the airline.

To solve the resulting problem, we use a penalty-based strategy. The method combines an outer loop that updates the reweighted approximation of fixed costs with an inner loop that alternates between a penalized bilevel solve and a lower-level solve, followed by projected updates of the market hyperparameters. This design keeps the computational advantages of the continuous model while introducing a principled mechanism to steer the network toward higher-profit configurations.

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the main modeling directions that motivate the approach. Section 3 introduces the airline hub-and-spoke setting and the optimization variables. Section 4 presents the single-level lower-level model. Section 5 formulates the bilevel extension and its penalty-based resolution strategy. Section 6 discusses the computational implementation, and Section 7 concludes.
