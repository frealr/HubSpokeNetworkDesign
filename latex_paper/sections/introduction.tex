\section{Introduction}
Airline network design requires deciding where capacity should be installed, which direct connections should be operated, and how origin-destination demand should be routed through the resulting network. In hub-and-spoke systems these decisions are strongly coupled: opening hub capacity changes the set of attractive itineraries, which in turn affects traffic concentration, operating cost, and profitability. This interaction makes the problem structurally difficult, especially when one aims to model passenger choice and fixed infrastructure costs within the same optimization framework.

Classical formulations often rely on mixed-integer models in which link-opening and station-opening decisions are represented explicitly through binary variables. While these formulations are expressive, they become hard to solve when demand assignment, congestion, and market share effects are modeled with enough detail to be operationally meaningful. The implementation developed in this project follows a different route. Instead of enforcing discreteness directly, we represent the network through continuous capacity variables and recover sparse hub-and-spoke structures by combining budget constraints, passenger-choice terms, and iterative reweighting strategies.

The resulting approach proceeds in two layers. First, a single-level continuous model balances passenger-attraction terms and operating costs under network and capacity constraints. The passenger side is written through entropy-based expressions that induce a logit-type split between the proposed airline network and external alternatives. Second, a bilevel mechanism adapts market-specific weights in the lower-level objective so that the induced design is better aligned with the true economic objective of the airline, namely profit maximization. Fixed opening and hub costs are incorporated through an iterative reweighted $\ell_1$ approximation that promotes sparsity in capacity decisions and progressively improves the continuous representation of discrete expenditures.

This draft organizes the methodology around those ingredients. We first describe the network design setting and the main variables, then summarize the single-level formulation, present the bilevel weighting scheme, and finally outline the iterative computational procedure used in the code.
